# The Gift Box Story

*[← Error Model](error-model.md) · [Access Control →](access-control.md)*

---

## Why provenance deserves its own field

It is Christmas.

Someone lives with their partner.

This person ordered a gift for the partner.

A box arrives.

No label.
No sender name.
No tracking slip.
Nothing.

The natural assumption follows:

> This is my delivery.

Why? Because something was ordered, and now a box has appeared.

So they go to open it.

The working deposit at that moment is:

* **P**: This box is my delivery.
* **S**: A box arrived; I ordered one; this must be it.
* **E**: I may be wrong about whose box this is.
* **V**: came through my usual courier, for my order — that is the best they can write.

Then the partner stops them:

> No! I ordered you a gift.

Now the deposit is challenged.

Notice what the challenge does and does not say.

It does not say **P** is false. The box may well be the recipient's delivery.
It does not say the standard is wrong. "A box arrived and I ordered one" is reasonable.
It does not say the error model is broken.

What it says is that there is a live alternative:

* **P'**: This box is the partner's delivery, not mine.

And the problem is that nothing attached to the box decides between them.

The recipient has written something in V. But whatever they wrote, the partner could write the same thing with equal justification. Both ordered something. Both expected a delivery. The V field is filled — just not with a genuine chain back to a source that only one of them could claim.

The truth exists — the box came from somewhere, it was addressed to someone — but the agents trying to form this deposit cannot reach that truth. All they have is inference, and inference does not distinguish.

That is what it means for V to be ungrounded.

---

## What happens when a second box arrives

Now a second box arrives.

Same size. Same weight. Same missing label.

Before, there was one box and one question: does it belong to the recipient or to the partner?

Now there are two boxes and the same question about each.

And because neither box arrived with any chain anyone can actually trace, the questions compound.

The recipient fills in V for Box A: their order, their courier. The partner fills in V for Box B: their order, their courier. But each is writing from the same position — inference from expectation, not a verified chain.

Neither can sort Box A from Box B by inspection.
Neither can sort them by weight or appearance.
Neither can sort them by looking harder at the deposit or at what was written in V.

The deposits were identical in P and S and E. Now the V entries are also indistinguishable — both assumed, both equally available to either party, neither traceable back to a source that selects one deposit and not the other.

Now there are two of them, and still no way to tie either one back to its source.

The issue is no longer only that one deposit was challenged.

The issue is that the deposits cannot be distinguished from each other at all.

---

## The redeemability path is bad

There is still a path to redeemability.

The boxes can be opened.

That will eventually settle the matter - once the contents are visible, each box is clearly its owner's.

But the redeemability path is badly shaped.

Opening the wrong box ruins the surprise.
Letting the partner open the wrong box ruins it for her.

So reality can still settle the issue.

But it settles it at the cost of risking the very thing the recipients were trying to preserve.

That is the shape of the problem when **V is ungrounded**.

Not hopeless. Just expensive to resolve in exactly the wrong direction.

---

## What provenance would have changed

Imagine the same situation, except the boxes carry provenance.

A recipient name. A sender label. An order number.

The problem dissolves immediately.

Now the box in front of them is not just "a box that arrived."

It is:

* a box from this sender,
* addressed to this person,
* tied to this order.

Once that trace is attached, the deposits sort themselves.

No one has to guess. No one has to open anything. No one has to risk the surprise in order to preserve it.

The truth was always there. The boxes always belonged to someone. What changed is that V is no longer filled in by the agent from inference — it is attached to the box by the system that sent it. The agent does not decide what to write in V. The chain comes with the deposit.

That is all provenance is: an externally verifiable chain that lets a deposit be grounded in where it actually came from — rather than in what the agent holding the deposit inferred.

---

## The workaround

Even without provenance, the problem can be solved.

The recipient and their partner each text a mutual friend what they ordered. The friend comes over, opens both boxes privately, and tells each of them which one to keep.

It works. The deposits get sorted. No surprise is ruined.

But look at what was required:

A third party. A coordinated exchange. An extra step that neither of them could take alone.

All of that work is doing the job that a label would have done in three seconds.

The cost of missing provenance is not always that the truth becomes unreachable. It is that reaching it requires rebuilding externally what should have been attached from the start.

---

## What this shows

The proposition in this story is entirely sensible.

"This box is my delivery" is a reasonable claim. The challenge is fair. The world does contain the answer.

What is missing is not intelligence, or care, or a better error model.

What is missing is grounding for V — a chain that comes attached, that the agent did not write from inference, and that their partner cannot equally claim.

The agents in this story can always write something in V. Free utterance means anything can be said. But writing something is not the same as having provenance. An ungrounded V entry and an absent V entry have the same distinguishing power: none.

Without grounded V, two deposits that should be trivially sortable become a coordination problem.

With grounded V, the same two deposits sort themselves before anyone has to do any work.

---

## Takeaway

Provenance does something no other field does.

S says how the claim was accepted.
E says what could have gone wrong.
V says where the claim came from — and why this deposit is this one, and not some other deposit that looks identical.

Without V, redeemability may still exist. But the path to it gets longer, riskier, and more expensive than the situation deserved.

The gift box story is not about an unusual epistemic situation.

It is about what happens every time a deposit arrives without a chain back to its source: the burden of sorting it falls on everything else.

# The Speed Limit Story

## When proxy V is mistaken for grounded provenance

A driver is on a road.

They missed the speed limit sign.

Another car is driving in front of them. It is moving at 50.

The driver makes the natural adjustment:

> This road must be 50.

Why? Because the driver in front seems to know the road. They are not being stopped. They are moving confidently. Nothing about the situation feels suspicious.

The working deposit at that moment is:

* **P**: The speed limit on this road is 50.
* **S**: A car in front of me is driving 50; matching traffic flow is normally reasonable.
* **E**: The other driver may be speeding, mistaken, or ignoring the actual limit.
* **V**: Another driver on this road was driving 50 without consequence.

This is not an absurd deposit.

The sign was missed. The drive still has to continue. There is no stopping in the middle of the road to reconstruct the legal record. In the moment, the car ahead is the best signal available.

So it is used.

That is what proxy V is for.

A proxy does not ground the claim, but it may still be the only available basis for action when grounded V is temporarily out of reach.

The drive continues.

Nothing happens.

The next day, the same road again. Driving 50.

Again, nothing happens.

The pattern repeats.

Over time, the deposit feels more stable. Not because the sign was ever seen, but because the world has not pushed back. The road feels like a 50 road. Experience seems to confirm it.

Then one day, the police stop the driver.

The officer says:

> The limit here is 40. You were going 10 over.

The driver explains:

> I thought it was 50. A car in front of me was going 50 a few weeks ago, and I have been driving 50 here ever since without a problem.

The officer does not need to accuse the other driver of lying.

The officer does not need to prove the inference was irrational.

The officer challenges V.

For the claim "the speed limit is 50," another driver's behavior is not the relevant provenance chain. The relevant chain is the sign on the side of the road, the road authority, or the legal record that establishes the limit.

The problem is not that a proxy was used.

The problem is that the proxy was later treated as if it had become grounded V.

---

## What went wrong

The observation was real.

Another driver really did drive 50.

The driver behind really did follow them.

They really did drive that speed many times without being stopped.

But none of that made the speed limit 50.

The mistake was treating a behavioral proxy as if it were an authoritative source.

Another driver's speed can explain why the road was believed to be 50. It cannot ground the claim that the legal limit is 50.

That is why the officer's challenge lands immediately.

The issue is not that there was no reason at all. The issue is that the reason belonged to the wrong source class.

For a practical driving decision, the other car was useful.

For a legal speed-limit claim, it was not enough.

---

## Why repeated success did not ground proxy V

The most tempting part of the story is that the mistake seemed to work.

The driver went 50 once. Nothing happened.

Went 50 again. Nothing happened.

Kept driving 50. Still nothing happened.

Each uneventful trip made the deposit feel more reliable.

But repeated non-challenge is not provenance.

It only means the error has not yet been tested by the authority that matters.

The world can fail to correct a bad V for a long time. That delay does not upgrade the V. It only lets the bad deposit become familiar.

By the time the officer stops the driver, confidence has grown, but the chain has not improved.

The V is still:

* someone else drove that speed,
* I copied them,
* nothing happened for a while.

That is not the same as:

* the sign said 50,
* the road authority lists it as 50,
* the legal record establishes it as 50.

Proxy V can be useful.

It can even be the only thing available.

But it does not become grounded merely because it has not yet failed.

---

## What provenance would have changed

Imagine the same situation, except the driver had seen the sign.

Now the deposit is different:

* **P**: The speed limit on this road is 50.
* **S**: Posted speed signs establish the legal limit for the road.
* **E**: I may have misread the sign, or the sign may have changed.
* **V**: The posted speed limit sign on this road displayed 50.

That V has the right shape.

It does not come from another driver's behavior. It comes from the source that governs the claim.

If the officer challenges the deposit, the disagreement now has somewhere to go. The driver can return to the sign. They can check the road record. They can ask whether the sign was visible, changed, missing, or misread.

The deposit is not automatically true just because it has V.

But it is now grounded in the kind of source that can actually settle the question.

---

## The repair path

Even if the sign was missed, there were ways to repair the proxy.

The driver could check a map application.

They could look up the road authority's record.

They could drive the road again and look specifically for the sign.

They could ask a local authority.

All of these are slower than simply copying the car in front. But they do the thing the other driver's behavior cannot do: they reconnect the deposit to a source with authority over the claim.

That is the cost of proxy V. It may be enough for the moment. It may be all that is available. But the proxy and the grounded source remain different, and an enforcement event from the real source can arrive before the proxy gets a chance to be repaired.

---

## What this shows

The proposition in this story is not strange.

"The speed limit is 50" is an ordinary claim.

The standard is not absurd either. In ordinary driving, matching traffic flow can be pragmatically useful. It may even be safer than behaving unpredictably.

The error model is also visible. The driver knew, at least in principle, that the other driver might have been speeding.

What failed was V.

The chain that was relied on did not run to the thing that establishes the speed limit. It ran to another agent's behavior.

That is not adversarial V. The other driver did not need to deceive anyone. They did not need to know the trailing driver existed. They may have been making the same mistake themselves.

It is non-adversarial wrong V: a real observation, attached to the wrong kind of source.

Proxy V is sometimes all an agent has. The structural point is that proxy V and grounded V are different, and repeated non-challenge does not convert one into the other.

---

## Takeaway

V is not "what made me believe this." V is the chain to the source that can ground this deposit.

Sometimes, there may be nothing better than a proxy.

A driver may miss the sign. They may not have access to the road record. They may have to act in the moment with the only signal available. In that situation, copying traffic flow is not irrational. It is a practical fallback.

But proxy V should remain marked as proxy V.

The world is not obligated to warn an agent before enforcing the source that actually governs the claim. If the proxy turns out to be wrong, the consequence may arrive before the proxy gets a clean chance to be repaired.

That is why the safe path is not to reject proxies altogether. It is to ground them when possible.

Another driver's speed can help an agent decide what to do right now. It cannot finally establish the legal limit.

The speed limit is grounded by the sign, the road authority, or the legal record.

So the lesson is not:

> Never use proxy V.

The lesson is:

> Use proxy V when you must, but do not let repeated success turn it into grounded V. When the claim matters, reconnect it to the source that can actually settle it.

The driver may have had a reasonable basis for believing 50.

They still did not have grounded V for 50.

# The Job Interview Story

## Why a convincing answer can be more dangerous than no answer at all

A candidate applied for a job.

The interview is scheduled for Tuesday morning.

Monday afternoon, the candidate's phone rings.

An unknown number. No company name on the display.

The candidate answers.

A voice identifies itself as an HR coordinator at the company. Apologetic. Professional. An internal conflict has come up — the interview needs to be rescheduled. They will be in touch.

The working deposit at that moment:

* **P**: My Tuesday interview is cancelled.
* **S**: A direct call from the company's HR, identifying themselves by name and role, is sufficient to treat the interview as cancelled.
* **E**: The caller could have the wrong booking; the display showed no company name.
* **V**: An HR coordinator called from what presented as a company number, knew my name, the role, and the scheduled time.

Something felt slightly off.

The display showed no company name. The number was not one the candidate had on file.

But the caller knew the candidate's name, the role, the time. They sounded right. The candidate believed them.

The candidate does not go on Tuesday.

---

## What the candidate finds out

A week passes. No rescheduling call.

The candidate follows up. The company has no record of contacting them.

The interview slot was still theirs. Nobody internally cancelled it.

---

## How it happened

A competing candidate had their own interview the same week. Sitting across from HR, they noticed a printed shortlist left in plain view on the desk — names, roles, contact numbers, scheduled times. An ordinary mistake by the interviewer. The competing candidate read it, remembered enough of it to act on later, and walked out of the room with the only material the call would need.

That was enough material to try.

The competing candidate did not need to push a threat they would have to follow through on in E. They did not try to game S either — S is the gate the deposit has to pass, not a field that can be argued into being weaker. The move was simpler: spoof V convincingly enough that the candidate accepts the deposit on its provenance alone. If it lands, a rival is eliminated from the running; if it doesn't, nothing happens. This time it landed.

The call was not a precision strike. The competing candidate did not know with certainty what the target would do. They placed a call that *looked* like it came from HR — right name, right role, right time — and hoped the surface match would be treated as enough. That is the shape of *this* attack: a pure V-spoof, aimed at a deposit the receiver might accept on provenance alone, placed by someone who knows the call may also fail and tries it anyway. Other scam calls layer on more — pressure in S, manufactured urgency in τ, threats that lean on E — but the V-spoof is the load-bearing piece here, and on its own it was enough.

---

## What E was already saying

The candidate had already written down the risk.

"The caller could have the wrong booking; the display showed no company name."

That warning was sitting right there. The candidate saw it.

But the voice on the phone was confident. The details were right. So they set the warning aside.

In the gift box story, nobody could even form a clean claim — the chain was obviously missing, and both people knew it. Here, the chain looked complete. The warning was there, but the answer sounded good enough that the candidate stopped asking.

---

## The verification path that existed

There was a path to checking V the whole time.

The company's HR number is publicly listed. A two-minute call to that number — not to the number that called the candidate, but to an independently verifiable source — would have immediately settled the question.

The caller could claim to be HR. Anyone can make that claim. What they could not do is make the company's published calendar show a cancellation.

That is the check V is supposed to require: not asking the caller whether they really called — but asking a source that has no reason to lie and no way to be the same person in disguise.

The check was available the whole time.

It just was not taken, because the call had already sounded right.

---

## Takeaway

The gift box story showed what happens when the chain is missing and everyone can see it is missing: nothing bad happens, because no one acts on something that obviously has no ground.

This story shows what happens when the chain is missing but looks complete: the agent acts on it, is wrong, and by the time they find out it is too late.

The answer is not to be more suspicious of every call. It is to check through a path the agent controls, not the one that was handed to them.

Anyone can say where they are calling from. What they cannot do is make the company's own records agree.

V is not just where this came from.

V is the chain that still holds when the agent checks it somewhere the caller has never been.
